Four Bar Mechanism Design for Automated Trash Disposal

Objective

The purpose of this project was to develop a four-bar mechanism that could automate the disposal process of a trash container. The goal was to ensure that when a user deposits trash onto a tray, the mechanism moves it towards an opening at the back of the bin.

Context

Four-bar linkages are commonly used for motion control in mechanical systems. This design required precise point tracking to ensure that the tray follows the intended motion path while adhering to spatial constraints. The pivot points were required to be positioned along the sides of the trash can without interfering with the bin's body.

Design Process

1. Initial Modeling in Math Illustrations

The first step was to define the linkage geometry and identify potential pivot points. A set of perpendicular bisectors was drawn between initial and intermediate points to determine the optimal pivot locations. The challenge arose when one pivot extended outside the allowable region, requiring a redesign.

2. Refining Pivot Locations

Instead of placing pivots at the endpoints of the tray, they were moved closer together, allowing for a more compact and feasible mechanism. By ensuring collinearity at a specific point in the motion cycle, the sum of two links equaled the sum of the other two, maintaining smooth motion.

3. Transition to SolidWorks

The final design was transferred into SolidWorks for precise modeling. The origin was set at the bottom-left corner of the bin to ensure accurate link placement. The linkage dimensions were extracted directly from the Math Illustrations model, with final link lengths and pivot coordinates defining the structure.

4. Error Adjustments

Minor inaccuracies were introduced due to coordinate rounding, leading to slight deviations in SolidWorks. To accommodate this, a small slot was added at one of the tray’s pivot points, allowing slight movement flexibility without compromising structural integrity.

Motion Study & Animation

The driving link was selected on the right side of the mechanism, as shown in the video simulation. A motion analysis determined that the necessary rotation angle for the driving link was 206 degrees. The movement was simulated using an oscillating motor set to travel 206 degrees back and forth at a frequency of 0.2 Hz.

Results & Final Design Demonstration

The final mechanism successfully moves the trash tray in the desired path while maintaining constraints.
