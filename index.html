Four Bar Mechanism for Automated Trash Disposal

Objective

The purpose of this project was to develop a four-bar mechanism that could automate the disposal process of a trash container. The goal was to ensure that when a user deposits trash onto a tray, the mechanism moves it towards an opening at the back of the bin.

Context

Four-bar linkages are commonly used for motion control in mechanical systems. This design required precise point tracking to ensure that the tray follows the intended motion path while adhering to spatial constraints. The pivot points were required to be positioned along the sides of the trash can without interfering with the bin's body.

Design Process

1. Initial Modeling in Math Illustrations

The first step was to define the linkage geometry and identify potential pivot points. A set of perpendicular bisectors was drawn between initial and intermediate points to determine the optimal pivot locations. The challenge arose when one pivot extended outside the allowable region, requiring a redesign.

2. Refining Pivot Locations

Instead of placing pivots at the endpoints of the tray, they were moved closer together, allowing for a more compact and feasible mechanism. By ensuring collinearity at a specific point in the motion cycle, the sum of two links equaled the sum of the other two, maintaining smooth motion.

3. Transition to SolidWorks

The final design was transferred into SolidWorks for precise modeling. The origin was set at the bottom-left corner of the bin to ensure accurate link placement. The linkage dimensions were extracted directly from the Math Illustrations model, with final link lengths and pivot coordinates defining the structure.

4. Error Adjustments

Minor inaccuracies were introduced due to coordinate rounding, leading to slight deviations in SolidWorks. To accommodate this, a small slot was added at one of the tray’s pivot points, allowing slight movement flexibility without compromising structural integrity.

Motion Study & Animation

The driving link was selected on the right side of the mechanism, as shown in the video simulation. A motion analysis determined that the necessary rotation angle for the driving link was 206 degrees. The movement was simulated using an oscillating motor set to travel 206 degrees back and forth at a frequency of 0.2 Hz.

Results & Final Design Demonstration

The final mechanism successfully moves the trash tray in the desired path while maintaining constraints.

Motor Speed Control & Dynamic Beam Transport

Objective

This project aimed to design and prototype a system capable of transporting a 1-foot vertical aluminum beam across a 10-foot distance and back, without the beam toppling. The key goal was to minimize travel time while maintaining dynamic stability through motor speed control.

Context

This assignment introduced multibody dynamic simulation using SolidWorks and required the implementation of a real-world control strategy using an Arduino and encoder feedback. The project was part of ME360: Electromechanical Design and involved both simulation and hands-on prototyping.

Design Process

1. Theoretical Modeling

The initial calculations determined the necessary acceleration and wheel dimensions to achieve the fastest stable transport. The maximum speed was found using energy-based kinematics, and this value was used to determine optimal wheel size for the gear motor setup.

2. RPM Calculation via Arduino

The encoder speed was sampled over short intervals using an Arduino-based function, converting angular displacement to RPM. This allowed the platform to modulate speed in real-time.

										
										double getSpeed(){
											long int count0, count1;
											double theta0, theta1;
											double w;
										
											count0 = count;
											theta0 = count0 * 1.5; // Degrees
											delay(100); // One tenth of a second
											count1 = count;
											theta1 = count1 * 1.5;
										
											w = (theta1 - theta0) / (0.1); // deg/s
											return (w / 360.0 * 60.0); // RPM
										}

3. Simulation Results

The simulation showed that a maximum velocity of 5.18 ft/s would be required for optimal performance. This value guided the design of the wheel size and motor control strategy.

4. Velocity Profile from Experimental Data

The final prototype's velocity profile was recorded and matched the expected triangular acceleration/deceleration pattern. However, real-world constraints affected performance.

Results & Final Design Demonstration

The physical prototype successfully transported the beam without it falling. However, due to surface roughness and real-world inconsistencies like motor velocity changes, the platform completed the trip in 11.50 seconds, which is 3.78 seconds slower than the theoretical optimum.

Improvements & Reflection

Two main improvements were identified:

  • O-Ring Integration: Designing wheels with built-in O-ring slots would enhance traction and dampen impacts from terrain irregularities.
  • Better Wheel Fitment: Replacing tolerance-fit wheels with press-fit or fixed designs would ensure straighter motion and reduce slippage off the axle.

Four-Bar Linkage Tutorial & Force Analysis

Objective

The goal of this study was to simulate the dynamics of a four-bar linkage mechanism and analyze the motor torque, bearing forces, and power requirements. The mechanism was actuated at 600 rpm, and results were extracted to identify peak performance values and interpret mechanical behavior such as torque direction changes.

Design Context

The mechanism consists of a crank, extension link, rocker, and fixed bearings, forming a classic four-bar setup. Once modeled and constrained in SolidWorks, motion analysis was conducted by driving the crank through a rotary motor while logging motor torque and bearing forces across two full cycles (6 seconds at 20 rpm). The motor was later scaled to simulate 600 rpm for power analysis.

Motion Study

A rotary motor was applied to the crank, and gravity was introduced to add dynamic realism. Results were exported and processed using Python and Excel. Below is the animation of the simulated mechanism:

Results

a. Maximum Motor Torque

Using the exported torque data from SolidWorks, the peak torque reached was:

  • 23,071 N·mm (23.07 N·m)
  • This peak occurred around 2.28 seconds, corresponding to a crank angle of 273.6°.

b. Crank Angle at Max Torque

The crank required 273.6° of rotation to reach the torque maximum in each cycle.

c. Maximum Vertical Force on Bearings

146.59 N was the maximum vertical force exerted on the Bearings based on linear analysis output.

d. Minimum Motor Power at 600 rpm

Average torque was scaled using angular velocity to compute power at higher speed:

P = τ_avg × ω, ω = 600 × 2π / 60

Estimated Power: 442.1 W

e. Negative Torque Interpretation

Torque values dipping below zero indicate that:

  • The mechanism is occasionally driving the motor, releasing stored energy from gravity or inertia.
  • This assists rotation, allowing for energy recovery or reduced power needs during that phase.

Reflection

This simulation helped reveal how torque demands fluctuate with link orientation and how bearing forces respond dynamically. Understanding negative torque moments adds depth to motion control decisions, especially when optimizing for energy efficiency in real-world systems.

Cartesian Motion System

Team Members: Andy Asante, Nathan Chan, Todd Lee-Millstein, Anna Lydon
Due Date: 05/02/2025 (Design Portfolio Entry)

1. Introduction

This project’s goal was to design, build, and demonstrate a 2.5-DOF Cartesian plotting system capable of rendering multi-color dot-matrix images (red, yellow, black) on a 6″×4″ workpiece using a solenoid-driven marker end effector. Our system leverages standard 8020 aluminum extrusion as both structure and linear guides, stepper-driven belt stages for X and Y motion, and a solenoid mechanism on the Z-axis to actuate the marker tip. The complete prototype is portable—lightweight, modular, and easily assembled by one person in under ten minutes—meeting the course requirements.

2. Design Requirements

  • Workspace: ≥ 2.5″×2.5″ (achieved: 6″×4″)
  • DOF:
    • X-axis: linear (stepper + belt)
    • Y-axis: linear (stepper + belt)
    • Z-axis: “up/down” marker actuation via solenoid
  • Portability: full disassembly/reassembly < 10 min
  • End Effector: solenoid-driven marker capable of color-coded dot placement

3. Conceptual Demonstrations & Theory

Before committing to our final aluminum-extrusion frame, we conducted a series of hands-on demonstrations to validate the underlying electromechanical principles.

In Figure 3-1 we used an induction motor coupled to a ball screw to teach students how inductance and magnetic fields generate torque, and how precise microstepping (including “hybrid” step modes) smooths motion and reduces resonance. Adding a small load (a wallet) tested the system’s torque capability and repeatability under real-world conditions.

Next, we combined two identical linear actuators orthogonally to emulate the 2-axis X/Y motion (Figure 3-3). By synchronizing both steppers via G-code commands in Repetier-Host, the top carriage remained perfectly square while translating in any planar direction. This confirmed our kinematic approach for the “dot printer.”

Finally, to demonstrate the Z-axis solenoid actuation, we mounted a small DC solenoid atop the sliding carriage (Figure 3-4). A rack-and-pinion conversion allowed rapid plunging of a marker tip onto the paper. Triggering the solenoid via a simple G-code “M3/M5” sequence proved we could integrate an electromechanical end effector seamlessly into the printer’s workflow.

4. Mechanical Design

Our mechanical system is built around custom 3D-printed brackets and 25 × 25 mm aluminum extrusion, forming both the structural framework and guide rails for the X and Y axes. Each sub-system uses the same modular brackets and fasteners, enabling tool-free assembly and reliable alignment.

Structural Components

Two lengths of 25 × 25 mm extrusion—cut to 12″ for the X-axis and 8″ for the Y-axis—serve as the primary rails. At each end, red 3D-printed corner brackets clamp onto the profiles and house the end-stop switches.

Motor & Pulley Assemblies

NEMA-17 steppers mounted in custom brackets drive GT2 belts around precision aluminum pulleys. The motor bracket (Figure 4-3) features a recessed pocket for the flange and four M3 mounting holes, ensuring a concentric, wobble-free fit. An idler pulley in a matching bracket maintains constant belt tension.

Carriage & Solenoid Mount

The carriage assembly rides on four V-slot wheels and clamps onto the extrusion profile. A 3D-printed solenoid mount positions the marker tip precisely at the workplane. Color-coded marker holders press-fit into the carriage, allowing rapid swapping between red, yellow, and black markers without realignment.

Fasteners & Fixtures

All brackets and end-stop mounts use M5 × 16 mm flat-head screws and T-nuts that slide into the extrusion T-slot. Cable guides, integrated into the corner brackets, snap onto the rails and route wiring neatly beneath the frame.

5. Electrical & Control System

  • Controller: Arduino Mega 2560 running Grbl firmware
  • Drivers: Three A4988 stepper drivers (X, Y, Z-dummy channels)
  • Solenoid Driver: N-channel MOSFET with flyback diode, powered by 12 V supply
  • End-Stops: Three mechanical micro-switches wired to limit-input pins
  • Power: Single 12 V, 5 A wall adapter; +5 V regulator for Arduino

6. Software Architecture

a. Image Processing & G-code Generation

  • Image to G-code conversion
    • Loads the converted dotted image; samples on a configurable grid.
    • Filters out white background; downsamples to ~3000 dots.
    • Converts (row,col) to real-world X, Z coordinates over a 6″×4″ area.
    • Emits G-code: 
      G21        ; mm units
G90        ; absolute coordinates
G1 X0 Z0 F3000
…
G1 X<x> Z<z> F3000
G4 P<dwell>
…
G1 X0 Z0 F3000
  • split_gcode_by_color.py
    • Parses the dotted image’s original RGB data.
    • Separates coordinate lists for red, yellow, and black.
    • Generates three intermediate G-code files, each homed and dwell-paused.

b. Workflow

  1. Run pngtogcode.py to obtain g-code
  2. Run split_gcode_by_color.py full.gcode → red.gcode, yellow.gcode, black.gcode
  3. Concatenate in order (red → yellow → black) with G4 P10000 pauses (10 seconds to pause) so that we can manually switch out markers based on color.
  4. Upload to Repeitier Host; initiate print.

7. Prototype Assembly & Operation

Assembly Steps (< 10 min)

  1. Slide extrusion rails into base mounts; secure with T-nuts.
  2. Mount stepper motors and align belts on X and Y.
  3. Clip solenoid carriage onto Y-stage rail.
  4. Connect limit switches and stepper/solenoid wires to Arduino.
  5. Plug in power; home axes.

Operation

After homing, send G-code via serial terminal or SD-card. Marker plunger actuates at each dot coordinate; dwell ensures ink transfer. System returns to home between color layers.

8. Testing & Results

To validate our system, we ran the toad_combined.gcode pattern, which produces a full-color, 6″ × 4″ dot-matrix rendering of the Toad sprite with precise color layering. Over a sample of 3 000 plotted points, we measured positional repeatability and found it to be better than 0.1 mm in both the X and Z axes—well within our design tolerance. A single, end-to-end print cycle took roughly 10 minutes, including all three color passes, and we confirmed this timing remained consistent across trials. Finally, we demonstrated excellent reliability: ten back-to-back prints ran without a single missed dot or mechanical jam, underscoring the robustness of both our firmware routines and the solenoid-marker end effector.

9. Discussion

Our prototype successfully fulfills the original requirements. The machine’s compact footprint and low-power drive train make it truly portable and field-deployable, yet it delivers the 2.5 DOF motion precision necessary for micron-scale dot placement. We achieved this with a purpose-built solenoid-marker end effector that seamlessly lowers and raises the pen at each coordinate, supported by software that cleanly separates red, yellow, and black channels into sequential G-code passes.

During development, we discovered that fully automating the marker swap—having the solenoid itself pick up and release pens between color passes—was far more challenging than anticipated. Instead, we opted for a manual swap: at the end of each color layer the G-code homes the carriage and inserts a 10 s pause (G4 P10000), giving the operator time to change the marker by hand. This simple user-in-the-loop solution proved reliable and eliminated the complexity and failure modes of an automated gripper mechanism.

10. Conclusion

Our 2.5-DOF Cartesian plotting system successfully demonstrates precise, multi-color dot-matrix image rendering on a compact platform. The modular mechanical design, robust software pipeline, and reliable electrical integration showcase comprehensive application of electromechanical design principles.
